In this document I outline the workflow which I have developed (along with updates I have made to it along the way) whilst conducting scientific research.

Finding papers

I save the bibtex entry in a bib file (generally in the project root along with the other latex files). This is because the bibtex-mode in emacs provides powerful search options, templates for adding entries, sorting entries, cleaning up, and much more. Besides, the bib files ultimately gets used in the report so it makes sense to group the two together and keep them in the same location.

[2020-10-31 Sat 13:37] I have made a org-capture template to make this process easier. With the bibtex key copied to my clipboard, the template automatically adds it as the title and the corresponding link to where the pdf is saved (I still have to save the pdf with the bibtex key manually).

First, I use good ol' Google Scholar (along with a plugin to enable my institution's proxy) to find papers. Once, I find a paper I open it and read the abstract. If the abstract appeals to my current research focus or is interesting to me in general, I download it and save it in a folder which syncs across my devices. Next, I grab the bibtex entry and create a task using org-capture where I save the bibtex entry, one liner on why I found the paper interesting. I add the location to the pdf on disk, I set the title of the task as the bibtex entry and I save it to my inbox (or relevant project org file).

Reading papers

[2020-10-14 Wed 17:41] Notes on paper being read or highlights made will always be in relation to the current research focus/goal. So, a paper may be read multiple times but will have different notes since they will be made with reference to different research questions. So, it's better to just save the paper and it's bibtex entry and make a todo under the project's org file. Log the notes whilst reading the paper under this todo and use these notes to write the research paper.

I read the papers on my iPad Pro since it allows me to highlight or scribble notes as I make my passes through the paper. And last, I go though my highlights and rewrite the highlights in my own words. These notes I make in a dedicated file named paper-{bibtex-key}.org file placed in my org-directory so that I can run rgrep on all my notes when I need to find something.

Writing notes

Here, I try to outline some of the things to look out for when reading papers:

problem statement
What is the problem the paper is trying to solve? why is this problem important? why should I care? This generally leads to a good material for the introduction of my paper as well.
solution
What is the solution the paper proposes? how is it better/compared to existing ones (do the authors try to show its worth by comparing to existing solutions)? why should I care about it?
results

What were the analytical experiments conducted and what were their results? what datasets were used? what models were used and how did they perform? how were their performance evaluated?

Generally, in my field these questions can be answered by reading the Results section, very rarely are results disclosed in the conclusion section. Note that falls out of the 'first pass' content so I may come back to this only if I need this information for the task at hand.

limitations
Does the paper identify its limitations? what future work can be done?

After writing down notes on the topics above, I try to write a one line summary of the paper along with my remarks. These are always guided by the research question or work at hand. This is also why I prefer not to make dedicated notes.

The "why should I care?" flavor questions will generally be guided by the research question or personal interest that I may have at the time of reading.
