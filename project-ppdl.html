The research questions of this project are as follows:

  1. What are the existing solutions that facilitate deep learning on medical images that do not invade the patient's privacy?
  2. What are the top performing deep learning models for medical images that can be converted to be privacy preserving?
  3. Additionally, what are the specific attacks made towards these systems and what is the present amount of data leakage in them?

DONE wu2019p3sgd

Paper describes a novel SGD algorithms which preserves patient privacy. A rather micro approach in contrast to the macro approach of distributed learning I have seen so far.

problem statement
CNNs can easily memorize some aspects of the dataset. When working with sensitive data, this violates the privacy of the patient. Models are also susceptive to model inversion attacks which can be used to reconstruct images in the training set using the model parameters.
solution

Paper proposes a novel SGD algorithm P3SGD for pathological image classification. The method is based on differential privacy and is able to reduce overfitting and obtain a level of privacy.

Past work in the field focus on protecting the privacy at the image level. However most pathological databases contain several images obtained from the same patient. The authors propose to achieve privacy at the patient level which has the added benefit of reducing overfitting.

results

Real pathology data used. Top performing CNNs are trained using regular SGD and P3SGD and performance analysis conducted. Analysis to show model trained with P3SGD is resistant to model-inversion attacks.

Task is detecting presence of glomerulus (image classification). Standard image feature engineering (random rotation, color jittering), change in brightness and contrast and standardized image size. Data distribution not mentioned. Analysis done over accuracy of model.

The proposed method provides a new way to regularize deep CNNs, authors have conducted analysis with popular regularization techniques such as Dropout and Batch Normalization. This is a novel regularization technique for neural networks specializing in pathological image analysis.

limitations
No limitations and future work proposed.
remarks
It would have been nice to see how P3SGD compares to other optimization techniques such as AdaGrad and Adam. Only accuracy was used, without data distribution it is hard to validate legitimacy of reported results. No future work or limitations. Good analysis done to show that their method prevent model-inversion attacks. The method was tested with pathology images, further research required to validate if it can be extended to other tasks.

DONE shokri2015privacy

problem statement

centralized data contains personal information such as images, videos and voice recordings. They are stored by companies indefinitely, users do not have the ability to restrict their usage. Recently privacy laws have been put in place to prevent exploiting personal data.

But this restricts ability of medical institutes to apply deep learning to medical research, or to share data with others for collaborative research. They do no benefit from large-scale deep learning.

solution

authors propose a system for distributed, privacy preserving deep learning (first paper to propose Federated Learning). Models are locally trained at individual medical sites and selective model parameters are aggregated at a centralized server. Most updated parameters are then downloaded from the central server and used to update the local parameters. The system is independent of the algorithm used, can accommodate any DL model.

selective parameter sharing results in a robust models which avoid getting stuck in local minima due to input from all participants.

results

system was tested with MNIST and SVHN datasets for image classification. Results similar to models trained on central, insecure data.

The system is able to minimize the data leakage drastically since now the leakage is indirect in the form of model parameters. The authors show how Differential Privacy can be applied to reduce this leakage further. A comparative analysis to demonstrate tradeoff between privacy and accuracy is shown. With increased privacy the accuracy falls however by increasing the number of participants and the quantity of parameters shared, the results are similar to standalone training.

limitations
authors do not present limitations of system or planned future work.
remarks
system not field tested, ie. with actual sensitive data from medical imaging. No limitations or future work was presented. The system achieve performance similar to standalone models only when 90 participants are present, this may not be possible. all analysis done using accuracy of models, without distribution of data we cannot validate legitimacy of performance reported. fault tolerance tests not performed.

DONE amir2017scalable

DONE aono2017privacy

problem statement

DL models benefit from large scale data however sensitive data such as medical data are protected by law. Thus medical institutes face several challenges when sharing data, cannot benefit from large scale deep learning.

shokri2015privacy proposed FL which tackles but privacy concerns. However the system does not guarantee full privacy by itself. There is a tradeoff between privacy and accuracy: sharing all local gradients improves accuracy but violates privacy and vice versa.

The authors show that even a small quantity of leaked gradients can be used to extract the training data.

solution
authors propose an improvement by using homomorphic encrypted (HE). All gradients are encrypted thus making it private, as a result all the gradients can be stored on the central server which boosts the accuracy. Since HE is used, the computation can occur over encrypted data.
results

promising results are obtained. The new system does not leak any gradient to the parameter server and is able to achieve same accuracy as unencrypted data.

The encryption adds communication overhead between participants and parameter server. Analysis shows the overhead is less than 3 times for MNIST and SVHN.

Accuracy tests are done on MNIST data and system is able to achieve same accuracy as unencrypted data.

The tradeoff between accuracy-privacy now shifts to efficiency-privacy. This is more scalable since efficiency can be improved by using more compute power, dedicated programming, hardware, etc.

limitations
no limitations/future work mentioned by authors.
remarks
did not use medical data to test the system. Used a MLP to conduct the classification and only reported accuracy without mentioning data distribution. Good computation analysis done, authors show that by increasing threads, the encryption/decryption process can be reduced. Authors propose two encryption schemes (Paillier-based and LWE-based) but only do analysis with LWE-based.

DONE chang2018distributed

The paper talks about cyclic weight transfer, a modified version of transfer learning. This is an alternative to FL where training is no longer parallel.

problem statement

"DL has been used for detecting diabetic retinopathy, classifying dermatological lesions, predicting mutations in glioma and assessing medical records."

DL however requires a large quantity of data for training. This is not always possible, especially for rare diseases. Training on such datasets result in biased models.

Sharing data to a centralized location is not always feasible. Data can be of really large size (high resolution images), legal and ethical limitations and preference of institutes.

Distributed training is an appealing solution to this problem. There are two types: 1. model averaging: separate models trained and their weights averaged every few mini-batches and 2. asynchronous stocastic gradient descent: separate models trained and gradients aggregated at a centalized server. Both techniques increase the complexity as models have to train in parallel and there is heterogenity in network speeds and hardware of institutes making it a logistically complex problem.

solution

Authors compare 3 synchronous learning techniques: model ensemling (ME), single weight transfer (SWT) and cyclic weight transfer (CWT). These techniques are slower than distributed learning but avoids logistic complexity.

SWT means that model is trained at one site and fine tuned on the rest, derived from transfer learning. CWT does SWT multiple times meaning each site trains the model multiple times.

results

Models are trained on 3 datasets: retinal fundus images, mammography and ImageNet. The authors compare performance of the proposed learning techniques to centralized training.

All proposed techniques perform better than a model trained on a single institute. In total, training at 4 institutions are simulated.

SWT performs better than ME, and CWT performs the best and its performance is similar to centralized training. No overfitting occured in CWT even though the model was transferred to an institute multiple times. Higher frequency of weight transfer resulted in better performance compared to lower frequency of weight transfer.

Analysis done to determine optimal frequency of weight transfer. Higher frequency adds to model training time, authors show that a lower frequency performs just as well as centralized training.

Analysis done for robustness of CWT. Tests performed with and without institute containing heterogeneous data: non standardized quality/resolution/size of images and different quantity of images per patient (representing real world data). CWT still able to maintain accuracy as variability of data increased.

Analysis done on scalability of CWT. 6000 patient samples randomly distributed across 20 simulated institutes. Authors ensure that the performance of model is no better than random classification if trained at single institute. Accuracy of model at par with centralized training when model trained at all 20 institutes using CWT.

limitations

All simulated institutes were sampled from the same dataset: this does not match the domain difference observed in reality. Authors addressed this by simulating variability: changed resolution/size of images or low number of patients with class imbalance. Authors propose to conduct future studies such that each institute's data is derived from a unique patient population.

Model trained to perform binary classification, require further analysis to determine the added complexity for multi-label classification.

Authors only studied CNNs, and wish to study other networks such as GANs, RNNs and autoencoders.

Authors address the limitation of CWT that as number of institutes increase, the total training time increases. Also, we do not control the hardware and network speeds of the institutes. At such cases, distributed training may be more feasible. Further analysis to be done to determine this threshold.

remarks

Really well written paper, hardly anything lacking. One thing, it would have been nice to see some runtime performance evaluation. Comparision of runtime and communication overhead with FL would help as we scale number of participants. This would help determine the threshold after which FL becomes more feasible.

Resiliance testing would also help. For instance, what happens if an institute drops out? Are there redundancies built into the system? Does each institute persist its model until entire training cycle has completed? Can training resume from the previous institute if current institute drops out?

Solution only addresses data governance issues, system still prone to security threats and attacks.

DONE beaulieu2018privacy

Paper presents a distributed learning platform which utilizes DP for preserving the privacy. There is no analysis done on the data leakage of FL, so this paper can only be used for RQ1.

problem statement

Several evidence exist on the effectiveness of large scale DL for medical tasks. Centralized data repositories such as eICU Collaborative Research Database and The Cancer Genome Atlas exist. But these centralized data stores involve a laborious task of deidentification of patient info from the images. The deidentification may also compromise the utility of the images themselves.

Distributed Learning acknowledges these limitations however they are not inherently privacy preserving and need to be combined with other solutions.

Cyclic weight transfer (CWT) proposed by chang2018distributed addresses the data scarecity at individual sites but is not privacy preserving.

solution
Authors present a new distributed learning system using cyclical weight transfer and Differential Privacy (DP) to address the privacy concerns of CWT.
results

used the eICU Collaborative Research Database which contains deidentified demographic data of patients in intensive care and the Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) dataset which contains 994 breast invasive carcinoma patients

AUROC for classification tasks on the two datasets is provided, performance is compared for the following: 1. centralized, 2. centralized with differential privacy, 3. distributed with cyclic weight transfer and 4. distributed with cyclic weight transfer under differential privacy.

In general, the performance of 4. is slightly lower than 3. which is similar but not equal to 1. 3. performs better than 2. The performance increases with increase in participants.

limitations

Authors identify that their testing distribution is unrealistic. To address, they propose a hybrid training procedure where a global model and a local model are trained. The local model can be used to capture local trends and biases.

Authors wish to improve the system such that it can handle sites with small amount of data which makes it difficult to extract meaningful information.

remarks

The paper addresses the limitations of centralized datasets and federated learning uses differential privacy with cyclical weight transfer.

No runtime analysis is done even though the paper claims that the communication costs compared to FL are significantly reduced (the parameter server is no longer required). Other evaluation metrics not mentioned, data distribution unknown so we do not know how well the AUROC metric represents the model.

Authors should have compared their results with that of shokri2015privacy to see how cyclic weight transfer is different from DP in parallel.

Authors only tested the system upto 5 participants, it would be good to see the max participants required to reach top performance. Also good to identify max participants after which the performance does not improve.

Authors used 5 participants with the largest data, which does not reflect the skewed distribution in real world.

The future work proposed does not make sense to me. How do they integrate the local model to improve the performance of the global? Isnt the whole point of FL to address the bias in local datasets but utilizing shared parameters?

Authors claim that their system is more secure but they do not conduct any privacy/security tests or comment on the matter.

DONE jeon2019privacy

problem statement

medical images are large and sensitive in nature, the paper proposes a new privacy and performance focused solution for DL with medical data. Medical data is protected and cannot be aggregated in central location. Institutes are forced to use local data to train which leads to overfitting since the datasets are skewed.

There is a communication and computation overhead with FL. In FL, the models communicate twice in each training cycle: once to download original model and again to download the aggregated weights. The computation cost comes from the fact that several models need to be trained simultaneously. Also, as the model becomes deeper, these costs add up so the methodology does not scale too well.

solution
proposed solution is to reduce communication bandwidth and maintaining computation efficiency. The system contains multiple participants and a central server. The participants train their models using local data. The participants only have the first layer (L1) of the model, the rest are on the central server. Participants communicate the output of L1 to the server where the rest of the model is trained using each participant's weights. The output of the final layer is transmitted back to all participants where the gradient is calculated. The gradients are sent to the server for backprop. The gradient of L2 sent to all participants to finally calculate the loss.
results
The communication overhead is analysed for image classification using CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. Two models ResNeT and VGG are used. The models are trained using the poposed method and Large Scale Stocastic Gradient Descent. The communication cost of the new system is noted to be significantly lower while achieving significantly higher accuracy compared to SGD.
limitations
Authors mention no limitations. They only propose that in the future the system is expected to be implemented in geo-distributed clinics.
remarks
Not clear if gradient is aggregated before backprop or if each gradient is backproped through the network. Unclear if the models are trained for multiple epochs or just once? The amount of data communicated varies with the model, we should conduct analysis using U-Net. The communication costs are not compared to that of FL where the participants communicate only twice. Is the quantity of data communicated lower? More analysis needed. Medical data was not used, data distribution not reported. Did not compare classification results to centralized training so we do not know how accuracy of model is affected by the new distributed protocol. The server can still be breached but since L1 is not present, model inversion attacks can be prevented? Can attacker breach participants and server and then carry out model inversion?

DONE vizitiu2019towards

problem statement
Differential Privacy (DP) requires changing the data in order to anonymize it, thus introducing a tradeoff between privacy and utility.
solution
Paper proposes application of homomorphic encryption (HE) to address the limitations of DP. The MORE encryption scheme is used. This allows limited operations to be performed directly on encrypted data. Thus models are able to train using encrypted data and labels. Encryption key is not required.
results
The accuracy of a CNN trained on plaintext and cypertext using two datasets MNIST and X-ray coronary angiography (classification) are compared. The accuracy of the model with cypertext is only marginally lower, it does a better job of preserving the privacy of data. However, there is a runtime overhead since model takes 30 times longer to train on cypertext compared to plaintext.
limitations
If attacker has high enough pairs of encrypted-unencrypted data then they can compute the secret key. There is a trade-off between HE and Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE). FHE is more secure but computationally more demanding. HE is simpler while providing "good enough" security, making it more suitable for deep networks. Authors wish to improve security of HE whilest maintaining performance in future.
remarks
HE addresses limitations of both DP and FL. Since the data is encrypted, model inversion attacks are no longer possible and no noise needs to be injected thus preserving the data quality. This also makes the communication between participant-server secure. The model is not evaluated in a distributed manner, need analysis of runtime and performance when applied to FL. Since data is encrypted, we no longer need a distributed system. Encrypted data can simply be uploaded to central server for training a single model (but do we have data laws that permit this?).

CANCELLED li2019privacy

problem statement
Existing FL work still vulnerable to model inversion attacks which can reveal the original data. Noise can be injected to prevent this (DP) but existing work only validate this on toy datasets.
solution
Authors implement a FL system for brain tumor segmentation and extensively test the system using the BraTS dataset. The paper conducts analysis of various aggregation techniques in order to deal with various momentum-based optimization algorithms and imbalanced data amongst the training nodes. Authors also study the sparse vector technique for a stronger DP guarantee compared to what shokri2015privacy provided.
results
Models evaluated for multi-model and multi-class segmentation task using the BraTS dataset containing MRI scans of brain tumours. Data collected from 13 institutes each with varying equipment and imaging protocols thus the dataset is heterogeneous and represents realistic data. The number of examples across the clients were also varied. Mean class dice score (DC) accepted metric for BraTS dataset used to evaluate model performance. The performance of CNN trained on centralized data and FL compared, FL model takes twice as many epochs to train (reach similar loss). Authors also analyse the effect of partial model sharing. Sharing more parameters improved performance. Partial model sharing does not affect the convergence rate and performance decrease is negligible when 40% of the parameters are shared. Authors also analyse performance by varying DP parameters. Sharing 10% model parameters vs. 40% showed better privacy results.
limitations
Authors want to explore differentially private SGD in the future. No limitations stated.
remarks
They claim to present the first FL system to be tested using a medical dataset but sheller2018multi came first. Authors commented on the distribution of the dataset. Overall results are similar to FL models trained on toy datasets. Authors matched the analysis to that of shokri2015privacy so a comparative analysis can be easily made.

DONE phuong2019privacy

problem statement
None, authors simply propose an alternative stratergy for FL.
solution

Rather than sharing the gradients of the local models with the parameter server, the authors propose to share the weights of the local models.

The authors propose two systems: 1. Server-aided Network Topology (SNT) and 2. Fully-connected Network Topology (FNT).

The SNT system provides security against a "honest-but-curious" server (honest in operations it performs but curious to obtain data) by not leaking any data regarding the participants and their local weights. The SNT consists of L participants and a honest-but-curious parameter server. Each connection is a separate TLS connection, thus a total of L connections are required. Additionally, the weights are encrypted using symmetric encryption before transmitting to the parameter server.

The FNT does not contain a parameter server, thus weights no longer require to be encrypted. Each participant is connected to each other thus L(L-1)/2 connections required.

results

Used MLP to perform classification on text (various UCI datasets) and CNN and ResNet on image (MNIST, CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100) datasets.

The accuracy and F1 scores are reported, solution is able to achieve similar accuracy to centralized training.

Runtime analysis of networks conducted.

limitations
No limitations or future work mentioned.
remarks

The FNT system proposed is similar to the CWT by chang2018distributed with security analysis conducted.

The data is randomly split amongst the participants ie. participants contain equal number of examples. Distribution of the data per site is mentioned. Medical data not used to evaluate systems.

Although runtime analysis is done, results are not compared to FL system proposed by shokri2015privacy. This would allow us to compare runtime of sharing gradients vs. sharing weights.

Authors recommend SNT when L >= 20 and FNT otherwise. It is unclear how the system scales since experiments are conducted using only 5 participants. It is also unclear which system (SNT or FNT) has been used for the experiments and how the two systems compare against each other.

DONE kim2019privacy

Adversarial networks applied to privacy preserving learning for image segmentation.

Problem statement

Privacy laws on medical data impedes the development of centralized, cloud based image analysis systems. This has the benefit of not requiring any specialized hardware on-site, immediate troubleshooting and easy software and hardware updates.

Centralized systems require a client-server mode of communication. The communication can be encrypted to prevent outside attacks but this cannot prevent cybercriminals to exploit the data once they have gained access to the system.

Patient data can be anonymized by removing the patient's metadata from the DICOM data (done by converting to data format which do not support metadata eg. PNG). However, the identify of the patient can be reconstructed using the contents of the image with up to 97% accuracy.

Federated Learning (FL) is another approach where the training gradients of the data is transferred to a centralized service rather than the data itself. This allows for training models locally on geographically separate sites. However, this centralized model cannot make predictions on test set without transmitting patient data.

Next is Homomorphic Encryption (HE) which facilitates training on encrypted and decrypted data. However it only supports linear operations (addition and multiplication) thus for CNNs which use non-linear operations, the runtime performance is suboptimal with added communication overhead.

Solution

Paper proposes a distributed, client-server platform. The input from the clients is passed through an Encoder to obfuscate into a signal containing enough semantic information for analysis. This signal is sent to the server for analysis and the results are sent back to the client.

Work is inspired by GANs (generative adversarial networks) consisting of three components: 1. an image encoder 2. a discriminator and 3. a medical image analysis network (CNN for segmentation in this case).

Paper claims to present the first client-server system for semantic medical image segmentation with identify preserving distributed learning.

Results

Paper reports the datasets and performance of models. Experiments conducted on two independent datasets indicate that the model is able to maintain performance on the target task while preserving the patient's privacy.

Using the obfuscating techniques presented in the paper along with existing anonymizing techniques may allow exchange of sensitive data across medical institutes. This can aid in heterogeneous clinical studies.

The proposed method is also able to generalize to encoded images not seen during training, something that existing work fail to do.

Limitations
Both datasets used for evaluation contained MRI images of adult brains. Future work will test the generalization capabilities of the network for a larger domain shift (eg. adult to infant brain scans or between different image modalities eg. MRI and CT).

DONE yue2019privacy

Paper presents time series medical image analysis which is privacy preserving.

Problem statement

DL and cloud computing has allowed medical facilities to offload compute intensive CAD tasks to external facilities. Time-series analysis has greatly aided medical imaging by using rich temporal and spatial features.

The data can be encrypted during transmission however require decryption on the remote servers before training which is not ideal. Homomorphic encryption solves this problem however existing work can only operate on a single encrypted image rather than a sequence of encrypted images.

Literature which studies the development of lesions over time exist however they focus on improvement of the accuracy of the model rather than reducing the false negatives.

Solution
Paper proposes computer-aided diagnosis algorithm HE-CLSTM to analyze encrypted time-series medical images using HE to encrypt image sequences. The algorithm also improves the accuracy and minimizes the false negatives by using a weighted unit and a sequence voting layer.
Results

The algorithms achieves accurate predictions while maintaining low runtime overhead - authors claim it is suitable for real clinical applications.

Performance is tested using two independent datasets, accuracy, processing time and AUC.

The datasets, their distributions and evaluation metrics are nicely reported. Paper also compares presented solution with the state-of-the-art. Detailed performance and runtime analysis has been conducted.

Limitations
The underlying principles are based in BFV cryptosystems thus there is added runtime overhead from the decryption of images. Authors want to reduce the computational complexity of their algorithm in future work. They also want to apply their algorithm to other formats of medical data. Analysis of algorithm on other medical datasets is planned.

DONE torkzadehmahani2019dp

Paper presents GANs applied to synthetic data and label generation.

Problem statement

DL has been used in medical imaging and human genome analyses. GANs learn the distribution of given data and can generate synthetic data with a similar distribution. However, the training itself requires access to said sensitive data. Also ML models (including GANs) are vulnerable to attacks such as model inversion and membership inference.

Differential Privacy (DP) is a common technique however very few studies have applied this technique to GANs. The few studies available only focus on generating the data and not the label which renders them useless for supervised learning.

Solution

Paper proposes a differentially private conditional GAN (DP-CGAN). It is a training framework which can preserve the privacy of conditional GANs using DP.

DP-CGAN can also generate the corresponding labels and uses a privacy budget accountant which allows for less noise without compromising the privacy guarantees.

Claims to be the first differentially private GAN which can generate labels in addition to the data with promising results.

Results
Paper presents analysis which shows that the network can generate good visual and empirical results on MNIST dataset. Tested on real dataset and reports a high AUROC, results close to accuracy of model trained on real dataset (but lower, which is expected).
Limitations
Preliminary experiments so far, authors wish to extend the model to generate more challenging datasets such as the CIFAR100 and CelebA/B.

DONE gibson2018niftynet

Paper presents NiftyNet', a platform for medical image analysis. There is no mention of data privacy so might be relevant for RQ2.

problem statement

deep learning has been used for several medical imaging tasks across different organs. These models are specialized based on the organ and the task they perform however there is substantial overlap in their implementation and software pipeline.

Generally, medical data is volumetric (3D) and enriched with additional metadata such as patient info so they require varied data loading procedure. Domain knowledge is required for data extraction and augmentation all derived from the task and organ of concern.

Reimplementing all these models is a duplication of work, prevents distribution of scientific work and comparison of competing models.

solution

NiftyNet is a deep learning platform to facilitate common medical tasks using deep learning. The platform consists of common infrastructure (such as data loading, data augmentation, loss functions and evaluation metrics) and networks used in medical imaging. It also contains a database of pre-trained models for specific tasks in the field.

Allows researchers to generate DL models for segmentation, regression, image generation and representation learning applications.

results
results for two task - abdominal organ segmentation and image regression are presented. I am not sure what else to note down here.
limitations
The platform is yet to support image classification, registration, enhancement and pathology detection. The platform supports a small number of models as proof-of-concept which does not contain state-of-the-art models. NiftyNet relies on users to provide the data, data partitioning and model selection. Plans have been made to support built-in cross-validation and hyper-parameter tuning in the future.

kaissis2020secure   related

Paper presents an overview of privacy preserving AI techniques used in the field of medical imaging. It also presents the vulnerabilities of techniques and attacks that can be made towards them.

Problem statement

Computer vision and ML have predominantly dominated medical imaging until DL was popularized. Release of large image datasets (such as ImageNet) has enabled development of models via transfer learning. These models have been applied to the field of oncology - tumor detection, genomic characterization, tumor subtyping, grading prediction outcome risk assessment. Problem statement

There is a need for sharing medical images for research. The privacy of the patient needs to be preserved, usually the norm is to remove all patient metadata from the image from sharing. There is no set standard for de-identification of medical images, different policies proposing different requirements. In general, there are 3 approaches - 1. de-identification (removal of patient identifiers) 2. pseudonymization (de-identification and replacement of identifiers with pseudonym unique to the patients but cannot be linked back to them in the real world) and 3. anonymization (de-identification and further removal of info to reduce probability of re-identification).

DICOM already provides means of de-identification. The problem however is that there needs to be means of re-identification of the image such that after analysis of the image, the results may be shared with the patients. To do so, some fields of the DICOM need to remain intact or unique to the patient.

Solution
Paper proposes a method for de-identification and re-identification of medical images. Certain metadata required for re-identification are encrypted and stored on the image itself. After analysis, the fields can be decrypted using a key.

Rise in the usage and sharing of Electronic Health Data (EHD) has been observed in recent years and expected to rise in next 10 years. This data is utilized for research to aid in faster development of remedies and analytical tasks such as data mining, classification, clustering, etc.

Sharing EHD is important, however the publicized data must keep the patient's information anonymized. Several solutions have been proposed each with drawbacks (the author claims this but does not provide any references). Fun example: Governor of Massachusetts was identified by combining voter registration lists and medical datasets.
